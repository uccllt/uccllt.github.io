Quick History

Modern Ghana takes its name from the from the ancient kingdom of Ghana, some 800 km (500 miles) to the north of present day Accra, which flourished up to the eleventh century AD. One of the great sudanic states which dominate African history, the kingdom of Ghana controlled the gold trade between the mining areas to the south and the Saharan trade routes to the north.

The region of Africa that is now called Ghana, formerly the "Gold coast", was colonized first by the Portuguese, who in 1482 built their first permanent gold and ivory trading post, called "Elmina" Castle - the Portuguese word for "the mine". This castle still stands today near a town of the same name.

The Portuguese position on the Gold Coast remained secure for almost a century, shifting their business interests from the export of gold to the export of slaves to work the plantations in the "New World". The region was then also colonized for trading purposes by the Dutch, and later the English, the Danish, and the Swedish. On the Gold Coast, these European competitors built fortified trading stations and challenged the Portuguese domination.

The next 150 years saw much chaos and change as various European powers struggled to establish or to maintain a position of dominance along the Gold Coast. Forts were built, abandoned, attacked, captured, sold, and exchanged.

The coast line of Ghana is doted with former slave castles. The British gained possession of all Dutch coastal forts (as established primarily by the Dutch West India Company) by the last quarter of the nineteenth century, thus making them the dominent European power on the Gold Coast. This final presence established the Gold Coast as an Anglophone region/country.

On March 6th, 1957 the Gold Coast achieved independence - the first British colony in Africa to do so - with Dr. Kwame Nkrumah as its first Prime minister. On the 1st of July, 1960 Ghana became a republic with Kwame Nkrumah as its first President and took the name "Ghana", as a reflection of the ancient and powerful kingdom of the 11th century.

Ghana spearheaded the political advancement of Africa and Dr. Nkrumah laid the foundations for the unity later expressed in the formation of the Organization of African Unity (OAU). He was a firm supporter of teh Commonwealth and the Non-Aligned movement.

In 1983, Jerry Rawlings was elected as president, but then through a series of coups he remained in power until the elections of 2001. Currently, John Kufuor of the National Peopl's Part is president and Ghana has a renewed sense of hope for the future.

Quick Facts

  • Official language - English (official), Twi, Ewe, Dagbani, others
  • Capital and largest city - Accra
  • Population - 21,029,853
  • President - John Agyekum Kufuor
  • Area - 238,540 km² which is slighlty smaller than Oregon (249,117 km²)
  • GDP - $54,330,000 USD
  • GDP/head - $2,601 USD
  • Currency - Cedi (exchange rate)
  • Independence - March 6, 1957
  • Religion - 69% Christian, 8.5% Traditionalists, 15.6% Muslim, 6.9% Other
