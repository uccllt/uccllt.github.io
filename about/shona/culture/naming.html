Naming in Zimbabwe

The main character in our Shona village is Chipo.  This popular name for girls means gift.  In Shona culture, people believe that a name “can shape a person’s character and have a bearing on future behavior” (Chitando 2001: 145); thus many names “have a particular meaning or carry a special message” (Moyo 2004).    Children are usually named by their parents, though a close relative may be asked to contribute a name.  Pongweni (1983: 2-3) list six categories of Shona names:

  1. zita remudumba ‘home name’: given to a child at birth
  2. zita regombwa ‘hereditary name’: used to name a child after a relative
  3. zita rejemedzwa ‘complaining name’: given by a traditonal healer to stop incessant crying
  4. zita remadunuhurirwa ‘exagerration name’: nickname given in adulthood
  5. zita rechihani: given to a child to mark an important event
  6. zita rekudzandura ‘plucking name’: name adopted to replace an embarassing name in any of the above categories.

Before Europeans reached Zimbabwe in 1890, most Shona names were “traditional and culture-bound” (Chitando 145; cf. Pongweni 2).  Functions performed by names included: thanking the spiritual world; making requests; complaining; looking to the future; addressing opponents; marking a special event; reminding people of circumstances surrounding either the conception or birth of a child; hitting out at one’s enemies; expressing delight, gratitude or disappointment; gloating over something; and expressing special sentiments over life’s trials and tribulations (Chitando; Moyo).  The contextual meaning of the name depends on who named the child (e.g. the mother or her in-laws) and to whom the name is addressed.

As missionaries entered Zimbabwe, many Shona people abandoned traditional names in favor of European, biblical or Christian names.   From the 1930s to the 1970s, most Shona who were baptized dropped Shona names and adopted biblical ones (Mashiri 1999 cited in Chitando 2001: 146).  Others were simply given European names by their white employers, colonial officers, or school teachers (Chitando 146-147).During the chimurenga, or liberation struggle, of the 1960s and 1970s, guerilla fighters chose pseudonyms in order to protect their families.  Some of these were deliberate critiques of Christianity, such as Judas Iscariot, Satan, and Devil.  Shona versions existed as well.

Diabhorosi diabolical
Teurai Ropa spill blood
Urayai kill

Ancestral and culture-bound names began to appear during the war as well, as parents living through the chimurenga named their children Shona names referring to that experience.

Hondo war
Tichatonga we shall rule
Simba power (sometimes short for Simbarashe, God’s power; a boy’s name)

Children of parents who are heavily involved in church activities are often given biblical names, such as Joseph, Josphat, Samuel, Rebecca, Nathanil, Jedaiah, Shemaiah, Shaddai, and so on (Chitando 150).English words are also common names for Shona speakers, such as Polite, Oblivious, Doubt, Most, Last, Evidence, Energy, Knowledge, and Final (Moyo; Chanetsa).  One also finds English names which are obviously translated from older Shona ones:

Due to the absence of written records before Europeans arrived in Zimbabwe, it is not clear when Shona speakers began using surnames.  Oral histories of the events of the 19th century refer to heroes and heroines by a single name (Pongweni 4).
