Grammar Notes

Ini ndinonzi ... “I am called”  is a common way of introducing oneself in Shona.

Chipo uses the first person singular personal pronoun Ini (I) to introduce herself.  To learn more about personal pronouns, click here.

The verb ndinonzi has three meaningful parts:

ndi  
no    
nzi
I 
am 
called
subject marker
present tense marker
verb stem

 



Each personal pronoun is associated with a subject marker.  The subject marker is crucial to the verb, while the personal pronoun is used for emphasis.  To learn more about subject markers used for personal pronouns, click here.

A noun that refers to a thing also has a subject marker, based on its noun class.  For example, take note of the different subject markers used when Chipo says:

            Zita rangu ... rinonzi Chipo Shumba.       My name is (called) Chipo Shumba.
            Chikoro ... chinonzi Gweshe Primary School.  The school is called Gweshe Primary Schoo.

Zita (name) is a class 5 noun so its subject marker is ri-.  Chikoro (school) is a class 7 noun so its subject marker is chi-.  To learn more about noun class and subject markers, click here.

Another way of introducing oneself is to say Zita rangu rinonzi ... (My name is [called] ...).  Here, zita (name) is the subject of the sentence, and rangu (my) is a possessive adjective.  Most adjectives in Shona agree with the class of the nouns they modify.  Zita is a class 5 noun, so its possessive adjectives start with the letter r-. 

            zita rangu         my name
            zita rako          your name
            zita rake           his/her name
            zita redu           our name
            zita renyu         your name
            zita ravo          their name

To learn more about possessive adjectives, click here.

Throughout the passage, Chipo uses the simple present tense, marked with the tense marker -no-,  appropriate for an introduction.

When describing herself directly, Chipo uses the verb ‘to be’:

            Ndiri musikana         I am a girl

Ndiri means “I am.”  It uses the present tense subject marker ndi- (I), but there is no tense marker because the verb “to be” is irregular in the present tense.  To learn more about the verb “to be,” click here.

The verb “to have” is also irregular in the present tense.  Chipo says, Ndine makore matanhatu.  (Literally: I have six years.)  She uses the present tense subject marker ndi- (I), but no tense marker.  To learn more about the verb “to have,” click here.

Chipo uses a variety of adjectives in describing herself, her school, and her classmates.

rakakwana

mupfupi

mutema

matanhatu

chakanaka

vakawanda

Some of these are simple adjectives, which add an adjectival stem in order to agree with the nouns they modify: 

-pfupi

-tema

-tanhatu

To learn more about simple adjectives, click here.

Others are verbal adjectives.  They consist of a verb stem and are used in the stative/perfective tense to form adjectives.

-kwana

-wanda
